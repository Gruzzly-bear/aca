SCP-9216

Item #: SCP-9216

Object Class: Thaumiel

Special Containment Procedures: SCP-9216 is to be contained in standard humanoid living quarters at Field Site W-8, and provided with a Foundation computing station with level 3 privileges. Any requests it makes for amenities are to be reviewed and potentially approved by Head Researcher Mary Vollard. Any researcher on site may contact Doctor Vollard to request SCP-9216's assistance with their current case file.

Description: SCP-9216 is a sapient organic biomass. The individual tissues and cells that make up SCP-9216 resemble those of a human, and it often maintains a nearly human figure. Its actual anatomy is highly fluid, such that its organs, their number, and their relative placement around its body are highly variable; it is not uncommon for fully-formed facial features to emerge from the dermal layers of SCP-9216 over the course of several minutes, move across the surface of its skin, and then disappear in a similar manner. SCP-9216 is able to influence these "fluctuations" to some degree, and it is usually able to maintain at least one matching pair of eyes, one nose, and one mouth on its face.

SCP-9216 is a strong supporter of the Foundation and its mission, and has been contributing to the containment of other SCP objects since it was first brought to Field Site W-8, leading to its current Thaumiel designation. As Field Site W-8 is a "first destination" site for many objects of interest after being acquired by the Foundation, it is not uncommon to discover that new research subjects harbor lethal threats during initial testing. SCP-9216, with its redundant anatomy and willingness to serve the Foundation, makes an ideal examiner for new objects processed at the site, and has saved countless lives with its service.

A full list of the SCP objects that SCP-9216 assisted with is available upon request from Site Director Graham.

Addendum 1: Biological test logs
Basic format:

Test performed
Observations
Dr. Vollard's notes

Test 1:

Dermal tissue collection
Organic matter was collected by Dr. Vollard via cutting a 0.72g piece of flesh off the subject's arm with a scalpel. No bleeding was observed from the wound, which quickly widened and converted into a nostril-like aperture in the subject's flesh. The collected sample was semi-solid, deforming readily like soft putty. Under a microscope, the cells making up the sample resembled malignant cancer cells.
There do not appear to be any of the more nasty pathogens that I might worry about from a Sarkic anomaly, which is a relief.

Test 2:

Cohabitation
In order to test if the subject has any deleterious effects on other organisms, a series of cohabitation trials were held, in which SCP-9216 shared its living quarters with various creatures of increasing similarity to humans. The creatures which SCP-9216 cohabited with (and their span of cohabitation) were, in order:
-Five lab mice. Time: 2 weeks.
-One rooster, one hen, and all eggs produced over the cohabitation period. Time: 2 weeks.
-D-3469. Time: 1 month.
After each trial, the cohabiting creature(s) were quarantined for a month. No creature displayed any negative effects from the cohabitation trials.
All creatures panicked like mad the first day. I can't say I think that is an anomalous effect. The D class, especially, did not like the idea of sharing a cell with a monster, but they calmed down when they realized it was a month of leave from regular testing. At the end, they even said they'd miss SCP-9216 — apparently, it was the best cellmate they've had at the Foundation. Hah!

Test 3:

Malleability
The subject was presented with a square wire mesh, with holes approximately 1cm to a side, and was asked to press their hand through the mesh. The subject complied, and was able to pass the mesh through the flesh of their hand. Then, the subject was given a wooden dowel, 0.2m long, and 3cm in diameter. They were asked to pass the dowel through the midpoint of their forearm, as a means to probe the rigidity of their internal structure. The subject attempted to pass the dowel through their forearm, but this caused their arm to detach from their body past the dowel. The wad of flesh immediately lost its shape upon contact with the table, "splattering" in a viscous, semi-fluid manner, and causing the subject visible discomfort. The mass of flesh surged back towards the subject after a moment, rejoining the arm and reforming the missing appendage. The test was aborted due to the emotional response of the subject.
The subject was unused to parts of their flesh separating, and quickly panicked. The test did establish that we have a subject who can pass through fine meshes, and it suggested they are malleable enough to squeeze into small gaps as well. If we want to keep this subject contained, it may serve us to earn compliance from them. That is likely cheaper than sealing off every crack in their cell so they can't slither out.

SCP-9216: There is not much to say. The Foundation is here and the GOC is all over this area. I needed some place to stay. That's all.

AMBROSE: That's all?

SCP-9216: What do you want me to say? How they gave me a room and started running tests? I complied. Of course I did. Every guard here has guns and tasers.

AMBROSE: Well, then, may I ask what you were doing before you arrived here?

SCP-9216: How long before?

AMBROSE: Let's say the previous week.

SCP-9216: I would prefer not to.

AMBROSE: … interesting. As a follow-up, I would like to ask another question about your past. Is this the physiology you were born with, or were you the victim of some sort of anomalous effect?

SCP-9216: W-what?

AMBROSE: Director Graham referred to you as a "sarkic anomaly" in conversation. That term raises some eyebrows, you see.

SCP-9216: … no, I was not born like this.

AMBROSE: I see. Can you tell me about how your body came to be like this?

SCP-9216: You're asking about something very unpleasant. Please, can we not discuss it?

AMBROSE: We can come back to it. Instead, can you tell me what you did with your life before your body was like this?

SCP-9216: Why are you doing this?

AMBROSE: We need to complete your file, SCP-9216. It is missing so much right now.

SCP-9216: Stop it. Do you think I'm an idiot? All these years and you can't even say what you mean to my face? I gave you bastards decades. Spit it out.

Ambrose puts his pen down and notepad down, and audibly sighs. Then, he stiffens, and stands.

AMBROSE: Pull your hands off the table. Now.

SCP-9216: Huh?

AMBROSE: Now!

SCP-9216 complies. Ambrose stares at the tabletop, and SCP-9216 follows his eyes.

SCP-9216: Oh, um, wait, this table is—

Ambrose speaks into a microphone in his lapel; inaudible. The door opens, and site security enters. SCP-9216 is escorted from the room, as Ambrose remains; the anomalous entity does not resist.

He waits for fifteen minutes before a field specialist in hazmat attire arrives in the room. The specialist takes several samples, and checks Ambrose for contamination. Until he is cleared to leave, Ambrose stares at the tabletop. Visible on the surface are two uneven handprints, freshly etched into the surface, on the far side of the table.

[END LOG]

Item 9: Summary of chemical findings

After the preceding interview, SCP-9216's cell was examined. It contains several metal surfaces with which SCP-9216 makes frequent contact. Each displayed signs of chemical etching consistent with exposure to a weak acid, such as dilute hydrogen chloride. The site employs metal door handles, and in areas which SCP-9216 frequents, the same signs of etching were visible.

Security footage from the interview was scoured, and it was verified that the table had no visible defects before the interview. The damage incurred was very shallow, only stripping the top layer of metal, but was still visible to the naked eye. Environmental testing determined this to be the result of a dilute solution of hydrochloric acid, with some organic compounds found in the human stomach mixed in.

There are no mentions of chemical excretions in SCP-9216's file.

Item 11: Interview 2 with Director Graham

Agent Ambrose had an interview with himself inserted into Director Graham's schedule in order to discuss what had been discovered about SCP-9216. The site secretary was told to inform him of the meeting as soon as he arrived.

Video Log Transcript

Start time: 9:00:14, 16 Mar 2108

[BEGIN LOG]

Director Graham's office. The director is sitting at his desk, staring ahead, when Agent Ambrose opens the door without knocking. He takes the seat in front of the director.

AMBROSE: Hello, director.

GRAHAM: Agent Ambrose.

AMBROSE: I wanted to talk to you about SCP-9216, as I am sure you're aware. Your cooperation will reflect well on you during the follow-up investigation.

GRAHAM: I'm sure.

AMBROSE: Indeed. Why isn't it recorded anywhere in SCP-9216's documentation that it can secrete a corrosive acid?

GRAHAM: I'm not sure.

AMBROSE: You are aware that this visit is a courtesy, correct? RAISA will learn the answer to that question, as well as any other question I might ask you. I am merely offering you the chance to offer it up and be acknowledged accordingly. I would urge anyone in a situation like yours to take advantage of such generosity.

GRAHAM: I'm not being evasive. I didn't know she could do that.

AMBROSE: Doubtful.

GRAHAM: H-hey, listen here, I am being serious! Mary said she was sure there was no danger to anyone else, and I trust her! Why the hell would I go asking her for— for a physical, or something?

GRAHAM: Well…

GRAHAM: Because she isn't stupid. She…

AMBROSE: Continue, Director Graham. You're doing a wonderful job.

GRAHAM: She didn't want the file to have… rough edges. Nothing to make people want to double check it. Secreting acid, that sounds like it would raise eyebrows. Not that many, but, you know, maybe one person would take a second look. So she pared back the testing logs to the things that helped the… ah, the story she wanted to tell.

AMBROSE: I see. As for yourself, director, why did you allow this?

GRAHAM: Allow… Mary is one of the finest researchers we've ever had here. She's given her life to the Foundation. Are you asking… why I covered for her?

AMBROSE: More or less. Regardless of how much I appreciate your cooperation, I am sure you realize this will, almost certainly, cost you your position. I also doubt that is a surprise to you. I am then asking why you bore such a risk.

GRAHAM: Agent Ambrose, I've gotten so much from my career already. More than I deserve. If it happens, I won't see it as losing my job; I'll say I spent my job to give Mary eight years of security. The woman deserves it.

AMBROSE: I see. That will be all, for now. Another agent will likely be in touch soon.

GRAHAM: Whatever you say.

Item 13: Interview 2 with SCP-9216

Video Log Transcript

Start time: 8:05:22, 17 Mar 2108

[BEGIN LOG]

SCP-9216 is led into the same interview room as used in Item 8 of this investigation, with the same damaged table in place. It stares at the handprints on the surface, before settling into its chair. Soon after, Agent Ambrose enters, and seats himself opposite.

SCP-9216: Hello.

AMBROSE: Hello, SCP-9216. I am hopeful this interview is the last piece of evidence I will need for my report, if you can just follow along with a few formal steps. Your compliance, of course, will be noted in the final report.

SCP-9216: Okay.

AMBROSE: Wonderful. Now, first, I suspect you have a Foundation keycard in your possession. May I have it?

SCP-9216: Oh. You're very direct.

AMBROSE: Is there any point to discretion anymore?

SCP-9216: I suppose not.

SCP-9216 reaches into its pocket, pulling out a card which it places on the table. Ambrose holds it up, in view the camera.

AMBROSE: Let the record show that this is the keycard belonging to Senior Researcher Mary Vollard. Now, SCP-9216, I am already certain of the answer, but could you state on the record why you have this keycard?

SCP-9216: Because it's mine. Is that what you want to hear?

AMBROSE: That seems to imply that you were previously in Foundation employ as Senior Researcher Mary Vollard. Are you claiming that to be the case?

SCP-9216: Yes, I am.

AMBROSE: That is very troubling, SCP-9216. Do you have proof of this fact? If not, you should be aware that impersonating a Foundation official is tantamount to an escape attempt, and will be treated accordingly.

SCP-9216: It's… there should be footage. Of the… the event. Check, oh, the security cameras from… August, mid August of 2011. Testing room 7. I think.

Ambrose stands and walks to the door of the interrogation room. He opens it, leaning out to talk to a guard stationed outside, and then returns to his seat opposite SCP-9216.

AMBROSE: We're going to have the footage combed. It should take a few hours at most to find this incident, assuming you are telling the truth. However, I would like you to tell me what we are going to find.

SCP-9216: W-what? Why?

AMBROSE: To verify it is you we will see in the footage, and not some other impostor, with improbable anatomy. I do want to say that compliance will reflect well on you.

SCP-9216: Oh, fine. Just… give me a moment.

SCP-9216: Okay. Alright. There was a new researcher, Evans. Pale, not even thirty, wispy blonde hair. He was anxious… I think he was only starting to realize what we do here. The new ones tended to come to me and Heinz. The other old boys were too intimidating, I guess. Evans was nicer than the others, always said please, very meek…

SCP-9216: Anyways, he came to me one day, panicking. He was assigned to intake, which was a mistake: junior researchers can't onboard new objects without a supervisor. That meant Graham f—… that meant Graham made a mistake, since he was still getting used to the scheduling software. I was mad, and just about stormed up to Graham's office, but Evans said that wasn't the issue. The kid had started unboxing anomalous objects, thinking he was supposed to know enough to handle it, and dropped something. He was about to puke, and could barely choke out that he chipped a statue.

SCP-9216: Technically, you know, he shouldn't have left the room. There are intercoms in there, and if anything happens, you radio out, and someone is supposed to come help you. Evans… well, he couldn't have known that. He was a junior researcher! He wasn't trained to do intake, so how the hell would he know what we do? I sent him off to decontamination, maybe raising my voice a bit too much, but I was focused on getting to the testing room so I could do cleanup.

SCP-9216: I rushed in there, and like Evans said, there was a half-unloaded box on the table, and a little marble statue on the ground, with a few shards chipped off of it. It looked Greek. It was this figure on a square base, and it looked so ugly. A bunch of limbs and flesh, it just screamed sarkic, and it was done in a classical, detailed style. Like Michaelangelo. There was something written on the base, and I looked it up a long time later: it was a quote from Heraclitus. "Everything flows."

AMBROSE: Did you spend a long time studying the object?

SCP-9216: Oh, no. It was a few seconds, but this was a memorable day for me.

AMBROSE: Alright, please continue.

SCP-9216: Well, I only had a few seconds because my arm started tingling, and then it started to bend where I thought there should be bone. My forearm must have already gone, by that point, and I wasn't used to holding myself together. I threw the statue back on the table, and I already knew this was very bad. Chipping the statue must have exposed something, I remember thinking. I also remember something splattering against the ground to the side of me, at the same time my arm felt a lot lighter.

SCP-9216: I didn't really want to look. You might think I was panicking, but I wasn't, though what was going through my head wasn't much better. It was rage. I thought, wow, this is it? Some anomaly is going to take the last things I had left from me? I am going to die in here, a puddle of… something, and that's it?

AMBROSE: You didn't realize the anomaly wouldn't kill you?

SCP-9216: No, not at all. Whenever I'd seen something that extreme happen to a colleague, I lost that colleague. I thought the same thing would happen to me. I thought those were my last moments. I saw Evans had left out his flasks, and one of them was decently high molar hydrochloric acid, so with the little time I thought I had… heh, it sounds so stupid. I grabbed that flask with my good arm, and I knocked it over, on the statue. At least it would be a draw that way: we'd both be slurry. Then my legs turned to mush, and before my ears went, I heard the sizzle of the marble being eaten away.

AMBROSE: Is that all?

SCP-9216: Oh, more or less. I was ooze when they found me. They put me in a container, and then assigned me to Heinz. They weren't sure I was still in there until I figured out how to respond to sensory tests a few days later.

AMBROSE: That sounds to be beyond what the footage will include, so you can stop there. What about this junior researcher, Evans? Was he affected?

SCP-9216: No. They checked the footage, and he was running for the door before the statue hit the ground. Whatever was in it didn't have time to disseminate while he was still there.

AMBROSE: Sounds reasonable. Well, that's the last box I needed to check to conclude the investigation. Thank you very much for you cooperation.

Ambrose begins to stand.

SCP-9216: What is going to happen to me?

AMBROSE: Hm? Isn't that obvious? You'll be reclassified properly, and recontained somewhere else. Likely with restricted privileges.

SCP-9216: Is there any chance I can remain here, and keep working?

AMBROSE: Someone with as much experience as yourself should know better than to ask that. Besides, it is not Foundation policy to explain our containment protocols to the objects we contain.

SCP-9216: After all I've been through, can't you humor me?

Ambrose sits back down.

AMBROSE: After all you've been through? Do you mean transgressing for eight years against Foundation codes that you yourself once followed? Or, do you mean creating a tangled mess that I have spent the past week teasing apart? If you wanted preferential treatment, you should not have falsified your file.

SCP-9216: You really think that would have made any difference?

AMBROSE: Pardon?

SCP-9216: I really doubt the Foundation would have treated me any different if I had surrendered at the start. I think they would've boxed me up and shipped me off just the same. There's an old Ethics Committee guideline about that: attacking staff should be punished, but the Committee recommended forgiveness, and lighter sentences, when there's a peaceful containment breach.

AMBROSE: … I have, perhaps, read that guideline.

SCP-9216: So, if you're saying it's not worth even thinking about me staying here now… there was never a hope. In fact, my chances should be better, since I've proven that I can still do the work like this, but you still think it's not going to happen.

AMBROSE: That was only my opinion. My involvement with your future is only to file this report.

SCP-9216: You could make a recommendation, couldn't you?

AMBROSE: Why should I? If I may be direct, you selfishly put this entire facility at risk because you wanted to pretend your life was the same as before. You compromised a slew of Foundation documents, and through this investigation, are wasting resources on cleaning up your mess. You seem like a drain on the Foundation, not an asset.

SCP-9216: You fucking worm.

AMBROSE: Again, you are not endearing yourself to the —

SCP-9216: You have the gall to say I'm a drain on the Foundation? I gave more to the Foundation than you ever will! No, I let the Foundation take more from me than you ever will, because I believed in it! My youth, my— my family, my chance at kids, no hobbies, no more love, no more sun on my skin, even the… the face in the mirror. Gone. All because I wanted to spare others. Because I wanted to keep them from what I had to face. I was locking up monsters, and because the Foundation made a mistake, and let some poor kid in a room with something scary, now I get caged up too? You don't think they should have shame? You wretched sack of shit. I gave more than my life to this place, and even when that life was gone, I kept giving, and giving, and giving!

AMBROSE: You compromised the entire site.

SCP-9216: What the hell does that even mean? I bet the Foundation won't change a single word of what I put in all those files I made, because it's damn good work and they know it! They'll just try and paper over me, so they can pretend they didn't just throw me away like a fucking candy wrapper when I needed them!

AMBROSE: SCP-9216, you are out of line! Be quiet!

SCP-9216 glares at Agent Ambrose, but goes silent. He stares back at her.

AMBROSE: The Foundation is not a storefront. It does not trade labor for recognition, or respect. It trades it for money, as every employer does. You tell me that you "gave" many things to the Foundation? That's called a job. You were compensated for everything you gave. In fact, you will be compensated one more time, as your Foundation life insurance policy will pay out to your next of kin as soon as the investigation concludes.

SCP-9216: You, you—!

SCP-9216 grimaces, and clenches its misshapen hands. It trembles, and pulls them close to its chest.

SCP-9216: Oh, this is pointless, isn't it?

AMBROSE: Pardon?

SCP-9216: Fighting. I really should know better. I know what I did when objects resisted like I just did: more bars, less amenities. Never more freedom.

AMBROSE: Treating anomalous objects like that sounds like a violation of Foundation guidelines. Thankfully for you, the Foundation will not punish you over transgressions from when you were in Foundation employ. Is that reassuring?

SCP-9216: You know, Agent Ambrose, you really are a model employee. Ah…

SCP-9216 looks down, placing its hands on the table. It fiddles with its digits; it has eleven, at the time of the recording.

SCP-9216: … this reminds me of when Henry died. I inherited all his projects, you know. It was a lot of paperwork, getting the names changed on everything. At the end, I had to meet with a RAISA agent to certify everything, and get the new cards. I remember he acted a lot like you. When I told him I was sorry for taking so long to finish, since I was handling the funeral, he just said, "it's fine." Not a word more about me, not sorry for my loss, nothing. I think I understand him now. He was just there to complete his job, and it didn't need him to understand or feel a thing about me. To him, there may as well have been nothing there in the chair opposite him.

Agent Ambrose shifts in his chair.

AMBROSE: He was likely from accounting, not investigations. A different department.

SCP-9216: He's probably been promoted by now. He seemed like a good worker, focused on the job. The kind the Foundation likes.

AMBROSE: I think we're done here.

Ambrose stands, pausing when he sees SCP-9216 is crying. It is staring at him.

SCP-9216: Agent Ambrose, I bet you'll be up for promotion soon. This investigation, you've managed it so well. I really had no chance. It'll reflect well on you.

AMBROSE: Perhaps. There's no way of knowing.

SCP-9216: Other people will ask you about this case, at least.

AMBROSE: Likely. Why do you mention it?

SCP-9216: Well, I just wanted to ask a stupid favor. If someone asks what the case was about, please don't tell them it was about SCP-9216. Just… say it was about Mary Vollard.

AMBROSE: I don't think I can—

SCP-9216: Please.

AMBROSE: … once it is revised, I will leave a note on your file about how you prefer to be addressed. Goodbye, SCP-9216.

Agent Ambrose exits the room. SCP-9216 remains seated, staring at the door, until site security arrive to take it away.
