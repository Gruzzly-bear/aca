Special Containment Procedures: SCP-9811 is to be strictly cordoned off from unauthorized access. Knowledge of SCP-9811's existence is to be restricted to personnel with a minimum of five years of experience. Exceptions may be made on a need-to-know basis. Additionally, given the memetic effect that the facility has on those who enter it, personnel may not enter SCP-9811 under any circumstances without prior approval for research.

The logo featured on SCP-9811.

Description: SCP-9811 is the Foundation designation for the "Montanna [sic] Natural and Cultural History Museum." The building has three entrances: Visitors, Guests, and Staff. Only the Staff entrance appears to be inaccessible. Foundation personnel have yet to determine how to access this port; attempts to utilize other anomalies, including SCP-005, have been unsuccessful.1

Inhabiting SCP-9811 are humanoids officially designated as SCP-9811-1. These humanoids act as employees within SCP-9811 and typically curate exhibits within the building. Blood, hair, and urine samples indicate that these anomalies appear to be standard members of the species Homo sapiens. None have been seen exiting or entering the building, and when questioned, they do not appear to remember specific details of how they arrived. SCP-9811-1 instances generally demonstrate a benign affect and are open to questioning. Through communicating with them, the Foundation has been able to construct a working map of SCP-9811.

Located within SCP-9811 are a series of artifacts (or "displays") officially designated as SCP-9811-2. These objects are organized into four exhibits:

  • Montanna's [sic] Own Monsters
  • The Wide and Whimsical World
  • Who and Where we Come From
  • From Lithographs, to Lenses, to Lasers! (Special exhibit; rotates quarterly.)

A fifth exhibit remains unfinished. There is also a small café located in the lobby.

An external view of SCP-9811 prior to shutdown, sourced from a review site.

Discovery: SCP-9811 was discovered after Foundation webcrawlers flagged a series of entries on a popular tourist attraction review site. Reviewers spoke of odd and unnatural occurrences within the facility, and a few complained about the apparently misspelled title. Foundation personnel elected to cordon off the building for initial review. After confirming the anomalous content of the facility, it was decided to cease public access under the guise of structural renovations.

Exploration: Based on the apparently low hazard level — determined by a lack of reported fatalities — it was decided that a lone surveyor was sufficient for facility exploration. Special Operative Tatya Mancy of the Department of Tactical Necromancy was selected due to her experience with archival work and her resurrective abilities.2 Her objectives were as follows:

  1. Confirm the anomalous nature of the structure.
  2. Document 9811-2 instances.
  3. Determine possible psychological effects, such as an unnatural lure (in the case of 9811-1 instance creation).
  4. Determine the purpose of the structure.

A series of two expeditions — carried out under the guise of journalistic interviews — were carried out over two days. Full footage and transcripts are available upon request. Included below are summary reports.

SURVEY REPORT 9811.I


Once more into the breach. I headed out at the crack of dawn to drive over. On the way, I received notice that I was being tasked to do a full survey of all four exhibits, including interviews of any present sentient anomalies. Looks like this report is going to be a long one.

It's fortunate that I've always liked museums. There's more than ever meets the surface, secrets behind every display. Also, a lot of them steal things. So every museum's got skeletons in the closet. And I love a good skeleton.

Checking in was easy. Wasn't like I had any weapons on me, unless you count pens as being mightier than swords. Despite the cordon, the 9811-1 instances (I'll be using "staff" for simplicity) were still in fairly good spirits.

On to the first exhibit: "Monsters of Montanna." The brochure advertised local horror stories as well as regional folklore.

Display Visual Description Plaque Text Summary
Those Who Walked Before A handprint cast, fur samples, and a coprolite chunk. Long ago, giant apes walked the Earth. A mass extinction event — perhaps the dawn of the anthropocene — claimed most of their numbers. Still, Montanna locals claim to see large creatures lurking in the woods, always just out of view…
Hunting Season A mounted pair of antlers over the static outline of a deer, surrounded by journal pages and newspaper clippings. Respect and inspect the woods when going hunting. If the deer begin acting strangely, act like you don't notice a thing and turn tail. Many a life has been lost to these mimics in Montanna's woods.

The handprint cast.

Fairly standard cryptid affair, right down to an article about a local named "Bobby." I wondered if the things on display were legitimate or just sensational. I can tell if something's possessed, but I don't exactly have a built-in Hume reader. The handprint was impressive, though, as was the account of retrieving it from the woods with a suspected den nearby.

As I was reading through one of the journal entries, a museum staffer approached me and asked if I had any questions. I decided to turn our chat into my first interview.

I wasn't quite sure what I felt leaving this exhibit. On one hand, I had evidence that the museum was officially anomalous with a capital "A," but on the other, a new layer of complexity seemed evident. One of our questions going into this review was how the museum might "lure" its staffers in. I'm not wholly sure that it does, or that there's a danger worth a Keter designation. It sort of feels like anyone who works here was always going to end up here eventually.

My last stop of the day was the on-site café. It was a fairly run-of-the-mill coffee spot. I noticed they had an entire rack of syrups, so I placed a fairly meticulous coffee order: oat milk, two pumps caramel, one pump mocha, milk foam. I thought the barista might spit in it out of spite, but nothing seemed off. Besides, what was it going to do? Kill me?

I sipped away in silence, reviewing my notes, until a nearby table called me over.

SURVEY REPORT 9811.II

I couldn't sleep the night before. I ended up trying to pick apart what I had so far. At one point I charted out the five W's:

  • WHO: Staff. Displays. Historical figures. Me?
  • WHAT: A museum. A trial. A creative space. Something else entirely?
  • WHERE: Montanna. Montana. This place is technically on a map, but is it this place? It's a microcosm.
  • WHY: Comedic retellings. Passion projects. Restorative justice. The love of the game. The thrill of the applause. Some inner calling. I can't rule out fate.
  • HOW: Ideas to drafts to displays. Staff curate and create. How did it begin? How will it go on? Will it cease to exist because of what we've done? Is that good or bad?

It didn't help much. I still couldn't pin down the purpose for any of it.

The next exhibit held some promise for answers, though: "Who and Where we Come From." The brochure detailed it as being twofold: about both Montanna and the museum itself.

I left feeling even more lost than before. It felt like even the staffers didn't agree on what the museum's true purpose was, other than housing their displays — which they also didn't fully agree on.

  • "Life's a…" => Clip of an anime episode where the three main cast members yell, "Beach!"
  • "He is not here! He is risen from the dead!" => Clip of an advertisement where a chocolate Easter bunny is shown, spliced in with a clip of an explosion.
What's That Racket? A small laptop with an attached microphone. Trying to figure out the source of a noise? This setup will identify exactly what's making a specific sound — accurate down to retrieving drivers license photos.

The laptop setup had a panel with sample sounds. When one played, the screen would display the corresponding image. Unfortunately, the included sound bytes were all orchestral clips, so the images were all of sheet music:

The selections provided for the 9811-2 demonstration.

Right On Sight! A laser pointer set atop a model of a large-lens camera. Struggling to line up the perfect shot? This attachment will focus on whatever you verbally instruct it to and help still your hands for the perfect photograph!

The last one left a bad taste in my mouth. I decided to ask the 9811-1 instance running the exhibit a few questions.

....................................................................................................
EXCERPT: INTERVIEW 9811.I ("NU")

NU: …no, I'm not aware of any military applications. I guess I can kind of see that angle, but I sure hope not.

MANCY: Generally not great to have that kind of thing floating around the public, just in case someone gets the wrong idea.

NU: I can't even imagine!

MANCY: I can. But, uh, let's not go down that route. How did you come to work here?

NU: I really like cameras. I wanted to do a display on it. That's, uh. [laughs] That's about it.

MANCY: That's it? No… no, like, deep thematic drive? No personal mission?

NU: Not really. The weird stuff is pretty fun to talk about, if that counts. My favorite is the section with the different types of lenses and how they work, though. It's basic, but I find it really interesting. Like, did you know that the specific type of glass used wasn't developed until the 1890s? It was called heavy crown glass, and…

There's going to be a point in that transcript where I just start saying "Mhm," repeatedly until the conversation ends. I tried to keep up, but I don't think my amateur appreciation of cameras can at all keep up with their sheer devotion.

At this point, I was at a dead end. I had one question remaining, and it was the most important one I'd been tasked to answer. I'd explored the exhibits. I'd interviewed the staff. I could've spent hours more speaking to every single living being there, but I had a sneaking suspicion that they'd only yield more contradictions. I also wasn't keen on incurring more hostility.

I thus decided to go to the last exhibit. Sure, it was empty. Devoid of anything Montanna or otherwise. But where else was there to go?

....................................................................................................
VIDEO LOG: EXCAVATION OF THE END

A long, dark hallway stretches past a sign bearing the text, 'Display Under Construction!'

MANCY proceeds past the sign and down a dark hallway. The walls are darkly-colored. Very little is visible in the frame.

Still frame from footage.

A single light flickers on. Then another, and another, in sequence.

MANCY: Okay, that's… [sighs] that's unsettling.

MANCY follows the path of lights. The space widens into a room. It appears more updated than the rest of the museum, but it remains empty.

MANCY: If something's lurking out there, let's get this over with. We can go out with a bang. At least I can say this place is completely evil and feel less bad about letting it die.

Ten seconds pass in silence.

MANCY: Die is sort of a pun, if you like that. Because of the necromancy thing.

Twelve seconds pass in silence.

MANCY: You know, I noticed about halfway in that I said it the same way every time. 'How did you come to work here? How did you come to work here?' I felt like a broken record. I don't know the right words to say. [sighs] They all know exactly why they're here. They all know exactly what this place is to them. So why can't I make up my mind? Why is it like a barrier I can't break past, it's just… just a mental wall.

Eight seconds pass in silence.

MANCY: Maybe I'm not cut out for this.

Five seconds pass in silence.

MANCY: [sighs] You could kill me, you know, and it wouldn't even matter. I mean, it's horrifying. Death absolutely sucks. It's the scariest thing I can think of. But it's not like I wouldn't come back. I'd come back, and I'd still be in the museum all the same.

A shadow casts over the ground. MANCY gasps and turns. Her breathing is shallow as GAMMA enters the frame and takes a seat.

GAMMA: [clears throat] I'm sorry if I interrupted something. It's a good thinking space, even when it's empty like this. Probably more so for it.

MANCY: I'm sorry for shouldering in here.

GAMMA: No, no! This place is sort of the museum at its realest. It's what we see when we all come in here. The blank slate.

MANCY: Pretty poetic view of it. You feel any better?

GAMMA nods.

Six seconds pass in silence.

GAMMA: Can I ask you a question?

MANCY: Sure.

GAMMA: How did you come to work here?

MANCY pauses before breaking out into laughter.

MANCY: Good one. Uh, well— there's a lot I can't say. I was just sort of the person to send.

GAMMA: And why's that?

MANCY: I'm okay at talking. And I used to do archival work. This kind of stuff was totally my dream. Maybe not Montanna, but curation. I've always liked all the stories museums told. I'd spend hours reading all the plaques.

GAMMA: So why did you switch paths? Was it just, like… intimidating? I can relate. I mean, you know why, but—

MANCY: No, no. I wish. Things just happened. You see something you shouldn't, when you shouldn't. You get pulled into things way bigger than you. And then— [sighs] I had an accident. I can't even really call it an accident. It was a fully intentional reckless attempt to do something good. And it worked, but something else really bad happened. And now I get sent to check out the spooky museums, and taste the coffee, and ask what all the problems are.

GAMMA: Gosh. I'm sorry.

MANCY: Yeah. Me, too.

Ten seconds pass in silence.

MANCY: Why is it called Montanna?

GAMMA: I couldn't tell you. It's always been that way. It's a real place, but it's not other people's real. It's our real.

Three seconds pass in silence.

GAMMA: Are you actually a journalist?

MANCY: [snorts] Not at all.

Five seconds pass in silence.

MANCY: Any ideas for this place? The exhibit.

GAMMA: Oh, tons. We might be shutting this all down, seeing as— [gestures] it's pretty empty around here right now. But I'm of the mindset that we keep pushing for it. Montanna's always gonna be here. The people that see it might change, but we're always here.

MANCY: Death or Sports?

GAMMA: The real question. [laughs] I don't know. I want to pick something that suits it. It's a whole new exhibit. It's this huge deal. If we blow it… then what? Who's gonna come back?

MANCY: If you nail it, who won't?

GAMMA: That feels like so much to hope for. I mean— okay, you're here. What's your perspective on things?

There's silence as MANCY considers the question.

MANCY: I don't know. I've been wracking my brain trying to answer as much. The one through-line I can think of is how much people seem to care. You're obviously half sick over it, the guy over at the special exhibit loves what he's doing, and the people over in the monster section are in some kind of cold war. There's a lot of devotion to this place. It's been impressive to see.

GAMMA: But what do I do with that?

MANCY: I don't know! Just— [pauses] Maybe it's just about the love.

GAMMA: The love?

MANCY: Yeah. Yeah! The love for all of this. The love is what makes a bunch of people dedicate their lives to making pieces about weird, niche shit in a place that doesn't exist. The love is what brings people back to see it again and again. The love is what set it all up in the first place. The love is what's going to guide you through the hours and days it takes to make all of this.

After a moment, MANCY sighs and pushes herself up.

MANCY: I'm probably projecting. But love is one of those things where— love's what brought me back in general. Love's why I keep running. Do everything for the love of it. And if you don't love it, find something you do. And keep doing it.

GAMMA: Do you love doing this?

MANCY: I do it for the people I love. That's the closest you can get sometimes.

GAMMA: I want do it for the Montanna I love.

GAMMA stands. They offer a hand to MANCY.

GAMMA: Maybe this is how it's supposed to be done. You've seen everything, you've formed your opinions… now you could help, if you chose to. Help us figure out where we're going next.

MANCY: It feels shitty to pretend to help. We're the reason it might not work out in the first place.

GAMMA: You might be the reason it does. We all wound up here for a reason. Maybe this one's yours.

MANCY: It was the luck of the draw.

GAMMA: Luck, fate, whatever you want to call it. You're here now, aren't you?

MANCY pauses. After a moment, she takes GAMMA'S hand.

MANCY: That I am.

I've requested that someone come by to pick up my materials. I know there's going to be a lot of frustration about this decision. It's what makes the most sense to me, though. What better way to gain the last bit of insight we need? How else do we keep this many people appeased after shutting their place down?

You could send in a team to retrieve me, but let's be real— just declare it another "Missing in Action." There are places I've caught a glimpse of that won't be on the map. I'm not sure they're even in the same dimension. It's not too bad, though. And if it is, well, we know what will happen. I'll be fine in the end.

Once more into the breach. If you need me, I'll be in Montanna.
